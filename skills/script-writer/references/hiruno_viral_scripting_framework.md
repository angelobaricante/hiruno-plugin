# Hiruno Viral Scripting Framework: Complete Guide

## Introduction to the Framework

The Hiruno Viral Scripting Framework is a proven system for creating high-performing social media content that drives engagement, growth, and conversions. This document outlines our proprietary approach to content creation, with special emphasis on analyzing and adapting successful reference videos while maintaining our core structural principles.

## Core Philosophy

Our framework is built on three fundamental principles:

1. **Clarity** — Simple language, clear structure, specific examples
2. **Conversion** — Strategic CTAs, value delivery, engagement triggers
3. **Curiosity** — Pattern interrupts, open loops, psychological triggers

## The Critical Importance of Video Length

**Optimal Length Principle:** Every video should be as short as possible while delivering all necessary value. This is non-negotiable.

- **Key considerations:**
  - If a 60-second video could deliver the same value in 30 seconds, it MUST be 30 seconds
  - Attention spans are decreasing; every second counts
  - Algorithms favor higher completion rates
  - Shorter videos have higher share rates
  - The first watch is the most important; rewatches are a bonus
- **Length Analysis Process:**
  - Study the reference video's exact length
  - Note which sections are compressed or expanded
  - Identify unnecessary elements that can be removed
  - Match the reference video's pacing and rhythm
  - Aim to be 10-20% shorter than the reference if possible without sacrificing value
- **Content Type Length Guidelines:**
  - Demonstration videos: 20-40 seconds
  - Tutorial videos: 30-60 seconds
  - Educational content: 45-60 seconds
  - Story-based content: 45-60 seconds
  - Yap / talking-head content: 30-60 seconds

Remember: The goal is maximum value in minimum time. Every second must earn its place in the final video.

## Framework Structure

### 1. HOOK (0-5 seconds)

The hook must immediately stop the scroll and create stakes. It should be under 10 words, create clear stakes, demonstrate personal impact, and generate immediate curiosity.

- **Hook Types:**
  - **Problem-Based:** "You're losing X because of Y"
  - **Pattern Interrupt:** "Stop doing X until you see this"
  - **Counter-Intuitive:** "This X is actually destroying your Y"
  - **Numbers-Based:** "These 3 things are killing your X"
- **Key Requirements:**
  - Under 10 words
  - Clear stakes
  - Personal impact
  - Immediate curiosity
  - **Open in medias res:** Your first spoken words are the topic itself — start as if the conversation is already running. Kill "hey guys, welcome back" and any intro throat-clearing. This is especially critical for yap / talking-head content, where there is no product or demo to open on.
- **Text Hook (on-screen track):** The hook runs on two channels — what you SAY and what is ON SCREEN. The text hook is the on-screen line displayed during the hook. It must carry its own curiosity gap that *complements* the spoken hook rather than repeating it word-for-word. (Example: spoken — "The reason you can't do this is that you don't have a framework"; on-screen — "the system nobody teaches.")
- **Tease the Named Mechanism:** When you are using a Named Mechanism (see Cross-Cutting Devices), name-drop it in the hook without explaining it. The undefined name is an open loop that pulls the viewer toward the payoff.

### 2. SELLING THE SOLUTION (5-15 seconds)

This critical bridge builds anticipation without revealing the solution. It creates curiosity, builds credibility, promises transformation, and sets up the principle.

- **Important Note on Flexibility:** The length and presence of this section varies significantly based on content type:
  - Demonstration videos may skip this section entirely or reduce it to 1-2 seconds
  - Tutorial videos often compress this to 3-5 seconds
  - Educational content typically requires the full 10 seconds
  - Story-based content may expand this section slightly
- **Interest Peak Types:**
  - **RISK REVERSAL**
    - Reassures the viewer that the video will be worth their time
    - Makes it seem like they have nothing to lose and everything to gain
    - Example: "And the crazy thing is this will literally take you under a minute to set up while costing you less than the price of lunch."
    - Example: "or your money back guaranteed, but you need to follow this video perfectly."
  - **AUTHORITY ENDORSEMENT**
    - Borrows credibility from an authority within your niche
    - Backs your logic with someone else's stamp of approval
    - Example: "This is a technique that I learned from Alex Hormozi that literally doubled my income"
    - Example: "This is the same way Nike generated a 100M in their Black Friday campaign"
  - **CONTROVERSIAL**
    - Either validates or challenges the viewer's opinion
    - Creates emotional response to grab attention
    - Example: "And yet, 99% of the people watching won't go to the end of the video"
    - Example: "and before you get your panties in a wad, hear me out"
  - **PERSONAL STORY**
    - Uses your own social proof to validate the video's value
    - Teaches through personal experience
    - Example: "And this is how you can learn from my mistake so that it doesn't happen to you"
    - Example: "This is the exact thing I implemented that helped me reach 1 million TikTok followers"
  - **NEGATIVE ASSUMPTION**
    - Addresses potential objections before they arise
    - Differentiates your approach from common methods
    - Example: "and no… I didn't use trending sounds, pay for ads, or even use hashtags. I did what's called [strategy]"
    - Example: "And before you say anything, no its not crypto or dropshipping. This side hustle actually works"
  - **HYPE UP**
    - Builds anticipation for your value portion
    - Creates excitement about what's coming
    - Example: "I was gonna gatekeep this, but fuck it I'll give it to you for free"
    - Example: "This works for anyone regardless if you went to college or not"
  - **CALL OUT**
    - Highlights what the viewer might be thinking or doing
    - Creates immediate connection through recognition
    - Example: "This is where most of your dorks fuck up"
    - Example: "first, don't try to justify your price on a sales call. Instead shut the fuck up and then say (solution)"
- **Structure Example:** "After [credibility marker], I discovered that [hint at solution]. Here's what nobody tells you..."
- **Key Elements:**
  - Keep it short
  - Don't reveal value yet
  - Build anticipation
  - Create authority

### 3. GIVING THE PRINCIPLE (15-45 seconds)

This is where you deliver the promised value clearly and actionably.

- **Structure:**
  - Clear steps or points
  - Simple language
  - Specific examples
  - Demonstrable solutions
- **Content Requirements:**
  - Use simple language (5th-grade level)
  - Show clear transformation
  - Provide specific steps
  - Include examples/demonstrations
  - Keep points concise
- **Delivery Rule — MAES:** Never deliver a principle point as a naked claim. Every point ships with at least one of — **M**etaphor, **A**nalogy, **E**xample, or **S**tory. Pick the mode that makes the idea *feel* obvious instead of merely stated. ("Sales is super important" is dead on arrival; the same point delivered as a six-second story or a sharp analogy is alive.) This is the difference between a boring yap and one people actually finish.

### 4. MAKING IT APPLICABLE (45-55 seconds)

Show exactly how to implement the solution.

- **Requirements:**
  - Immediately actionable
  - Simple to follow
  - Clear results
  - Specific next steps
- **Implementation Format:**
  - "Here's what to do..."
  - "Try this right now..."
  - "Start with this simple step..."
  - "implement this today by..."

### 5. CALL TO ACTION (55-60 seconds)

Create clear next steps that provide additional value.

- **Structure:**
  1. Offer specific resource
  2. Use keyword comment trigger
  3. Promise clear benefit
  4. Create urgency
- **CTA Format:** "I've created [specific resource] with [clear benefit]. Comment [KEYWORD] and I'll send it to you"

## Cross-Cutting Devices

These devices do not belong to a single numbered section — they operate across the whole script and are what separate a flat yap from a high-retention one.

### Named Mechanism (The "Recipe")

Give your method a proprietary name no one has heard before ("Triangle of Yaps," "STARR Method," "Hair Care Parity Logic," "Want/But/Until"). The named mechanism works on three beats:

- **Tease** it in the hook, unexplained.
- **Spine:** use it as the structure of your principle section — the name *is* the roadmap.
- **Payoff:** resolve and recap it at the end.

The unexplained name is an open loop: viewers stay to decode what it means. A name nobody has heard also signals proprietary value, which raises perceived authority. (Note that the two reference styles behind this update — the "Triangle of Yaps" and "STARR" videos — are themselves named mechanisms; the name is the reason you watch to the end.)

### Retention Layer (Open-Loop Maintenance)

Our Core Philosophy calls for open loops, but the five-part structure parks most engagement machinery at the front (hook + interest peak) and the end (CTA). The middle is where viewers swipe. Schedule a micro re-hook at each attention dip — typically at section transitions or roughly every 8-10 seconds:

- A forward-reference ("the last one is the one nobody actually does")
- A save / comment nudge placed at a natural payoff ("if you made it this far, comment [WORD] and I got you")
- A tease tied to the named mechanism ("this next part is the whole reason it's called [name]")

Front and back aren't enough. Hold the middle.

### Dual-Track Output

Every finished script specifies two tracks in parallel:

- **Spoken** — what the creator says to camera.
- **On-screen** — text overlays and visual cues, including the Text Hook.

Write scripts so both tracks are explicit (a two-column or clearly labeled layout). The on-screen track does independent work: it reinforces retention, carries secondary curiosity gaps, and surfaces the named mechanism and step numbers visually.

## Reference Video Analysis Process

When adapting our framework to match successful viral videos, follow this systematic approach:

**1. Structural Analysis**

- Identify the exact hook structure
- Note the timing of each section
- Observe transitions between segments
- Document visual elements and their placement
- Measure exact video length and section proportions

**2. Hook Deconstruction**

- What type of hook do they use?
- How many words?
- What stakes do they establish?
- What curiosity gap do they create?
- Do they open in medias res or with an intro?
- What is the on-screen text hook, and does it carry a separate curiosity gap?

**3. Interest Peak Identification**

- Which of the 7 Interest Peak types do they use?
- How do they build credibility?
- How do they create anticipation?
- What transition phrases do they use?
- Is this section present at all? If not, why does it work without it?

**4. Value Delivery Analysis**

- How is information structured?
- What visual aids support the content?
- How complex is the language?
- What examples do they provide?
- Which MAES mode(s) do they use to make each point land?
- Is there a named mechanism? How is it teased and paid off?
- How quickly do they get to the main value?

**5. CTA Examination**

- What specific resource do they offer?
- What trigger word do they use?
- How do they create urgency?
- What benefit do they promise?
- How much time is dedicated to the CTA?

## Section Flexibility Analysis

Different types of viral videos modify our framework in specific ways. It's crucial to identify these patterns:

**1. Demonstration Videos**

- Often skip or minimize the "Selling the Solution" section
- Jump directly from hook to demonstration
- Example: "This AI glasses are insane (I've been using them this whole video)" - cuts directly to showing the glasses after the hook
- Why it works: The demonstration itself creates curiosity and builds value

**2. Tutorial Videos**

- May compress "Selling the Solution" to 2-3 seconds
- Expand the "Giving the Principle" section
- Example: "Stop looking at the camera when you film content" - brief credibility statement then straight to the technique
- Why it works: The value is in the specific steps, not the setup

**3. Educational Content**

- Typically uses the full framework
- Balanced proportions between sections
- Example: "3 phrases that are killing your content" - uses complete structure with proper transitions
- Why it works: Needs proper context and credibility before delivering insights

**4. Transformation Content**

- Often expands "Selling the Solution" slightly
- Uses before/after demonstrations heavily
- Example: "This video got us 40.2 million views" - spends time establishing the transformation
- Why it works: The contrast between before/after creates the value

**5. Yap / Talking-Head Content**

- No product or demo to show, so value rides entirely on delivery
- "Selling the Solution" usually collapses to a single Achievement line (Authority Endorsement or Personal Story)
- Hook leans on Open-in-medias-res + a Named Mechanism tease
- Principle is pure MAES, structured by the named mechanism
- The Retention Layer carries the middle; without a demo there's nothing else holding attention
- Length: 30-60 seconds
- Example: "The Triangle of Yaps" / "STARR Method" style videos
- Why it works: the structure and named mechanism manufacture the curiosity that a product demo would otherwise provide

## Adaptation Guidelines

When adapting a reference video:

**1. Maintain Core Structure**

- Keep the 5-part framework as a foundation
- Preserve timing proportions from the reference video
- Follow the psychological flow
- Match the exact length if the reference video is performing well

**2. Adopt Successful Elements**

- Use similar hook patterns
- Mimic effective Interest Peaks
- Adapt visual presentation styles
- Incorporate successful transitions
- Copy the section compression/expansion patterns

**3. Customize Content**

- Insert your unique expertise
- Adapt examples to your niche
- Maintain your authentic voice
- Offer your specific resources

**4. Enhance Where Possible**

- Improve clarity where the reference video is weak
- Strengthen CTAs if needed
- Add better examples if possible
- Optimize for your specific audience
- Reduce length if possible without sacrificing value

## Implementation Process

**1. Study Reference Video**

- Watch multiple times
- Transcribe if necessary
- Note timestamps for each section
- Identify key phrases and transitions
- Record exact video length and section timing

**2. Create Outline**

- Map the 5-part framework
- Insert Interest Peak type
- Name the mechanism (if using one) and plan where it's teased and paid off
- Plan visual elements and the on-screen text track
- Prepare examples
- Plan for section compression/expansion based on reference

**3. Draft Script**

- Write hook (under 10 words), opening in medias res
- Write the on-screen text hook
- Craft Interest Peak section (or decide to minimize/skip based on reference)
- Develop clear value points, applying a MAES device to each
- Place retention re-hooks at section transitions
- Create implementation steps
- Design compelling CTA
- Aim for optimal brevity throughout

**4. Review and Optimize**

- Check language simplicity
- Confirm each principle point carries a MAES device
- Confirm the named mechanism is teased in the hook and paid off at the end
- Confirm the on-screen text track is present and pulling its weight
- Confirm at least one mid-roll re-hook holds the middle
- Ensure clear transitions
- Verify timing proportions
- Confirm resource value
- Cut any unnecessary words or concepts

**5. Test and Iterate**

- Track performance metrics
- Note engagement patterns
- Identify successful elements
- Refine for future content
- Compare completion rates to reference video

## Conclusion

The Hiruno Viral Scripting Framework provides a proven structure for creating high-performing content. By systematically analyzing reference videos and adapting their successful elements while maintaining our core framework, we can consistently produce content that drives engagement, growth, and conversions.

Remember these critical principles:

1. Every video should be as short as possible while delivering all necessary value
2. Study the exact length and section proportions of reference videos
3. Be willing to compress or even eliminate sections based on content type
4. The "Selling the Solution" section is often modified or skipped in demonstration videos
5. Match the pacing and rhythm of successful reference videos
6. Always aim to be more concise than your references if possible
7. Wrap every principle point in a MAES device (metaphor, analogy, example, or story)
8. Name your mechanism and treat it as an open loop — tease it, build on it, pay it off
9. Hold the middle with a retention layer, not just the hook and the CTA
10. Script both tracks — what you say and what's on screen

The key to success is maintaining the psychological principles that drive viewer behavior while adapting the presentation to match what's currently working in the market.
